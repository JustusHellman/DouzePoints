The History and Evolution of Eurovision

The Eurovision Song Contest was established by the European Broadcasting Union (EBU) in 1956 as a way to bring war-torn Europe together through a live, simultaneous transnational broadcast. What started with just seven participating countries has grown into a massive international event with over 40 nations competing for the legendary glass microphone trophy.

Over the decades, the contest has been a pioneer in broadcasting technology. From the introduction of color television to the implementation of complex satellite links and the modern era of high-definition, multi-camera spectaculars, Eurovision has always pushed the boundaries of what is possible in live entertainment. It has also been a mirror of social and political changes in Europe, reflecting the fall of the Iron Curtain, the expansion of the European Union, and the increasing importance of diversity and inclusion.

Musically, Eurovision has evolved from its roots in the "chanson" and orchestral ballad traditions to encompass every imaginable genre, including pop, rock, heavy metal, folk, electronic, and experimental music. It has launched the careers of global superstars like ABBA, Celine Dion, Julio Iglesias, and more recently, Måneskin and Rosa Linn.

Memorable Winners and Iconic Moments

Throughout its long history, Eurovision has produced moments that have become part of global pop culture. ABBA's victory in 1974 with "Waterloo" is perhaps the most famous, marking the beginning of their journey to becoming one of the best-selling music acts of all time. Celine Dion's win for Switzerland in 1988 with "Ne partez pas sans moi" similarly catapulted her to international superstardom.

In more recent years, the contest has continued to produce iconic winners. Loreen's "Euphoria" in 2012 became a massive hit across Europe, and her second win in 2023 with "Tattoo" made her the first woman to win the contest twice. Conchita Wurst's victory in 2014 with "Rise Like a Phoenix" was a powerful statement of diversity and acceptance, while Netta's "Toy" in 2018 brought a unique, modern sound to the Eurovision stage.

The Unique Eurovision Voting System

The voting system is one of the most exciting and debated aspects of the contest. Since 1975, the famous "douze points" (12 points) system has been in place, where each country awards points to its favorite entries. Today, the results are determined by a combination of professional juries and public televoting, ensuring a balance between musical expertise and popular appeal.

The "voting sequence" at the end of the Grand Final is a high-stakes television event in itself, as spokespersons from across Europe (and beyond) deliver their country's results live. This process has created countless dramatic finishes and has become a beloved tradition for fans worldwide. At Douze Points, we celebrate this tradition by making "12 points" the ultimate goal in all our daily games.
